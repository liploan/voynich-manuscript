Stephen Bax's Phonetic Decipherment

2014 Hypothesis

The late linguist Stephen Bax proposed that the Voynich Manuscript is not a hoax or cipher, but rather a text written in a real, possibly lost or hybrid language, using an adapted phonetic script. By identifying proper names next to drawings (such as plants and constellations), Bax established a partial alphabet of about a dozen signs.

Voynich Researchers & Methodological Debates

The Voynich Manuscript (MS 408) has resisted translation for centuries, spawning diverse linguistic, cryptological, and botanical hypotheses. Below is an overview of the primary active theories, their empirical arguments, and the academic criticisms they face.

Stephen Bax

Professor of Applied Linguistics
Phonetic Script Model

Approach & Contributions

Analyzed the manuscript as a natural, phonetic language rather than a code. Reconstructed phonetic sound values for individual letters by matching proper nouns adjacent to illustrations (e.g. Coriander, Taurus) using Semitic, Arabic, and Caucasus scripts.

Merits & Arguments
  • Targets proper nouns which possess high semantic stability.
  • Avoids arbitrary monoalphabetic substitutions.
  • Phonetic readings match historical Middle Eastern script variations.
Criticisms & Objections
  • Extremely small sample size (only ~10 confirmed words).
  • Phonetic mappings fail to scale to translate larger passages.
  • Introduces arbitrary phonemes to bridge undeciphered gaps.

Edith Sherwood, Ph.D.

Chemist & Independent Researcher
Tuscan Anagram Theory

Approach & Contributions

Argues that the manuscript labels are single-word anagrams written in 15th-century Tuscan Italian, using Dante's 21-letter alphabet (omitting J, K, W, X, Y). Mapped 122 botanical anagram decodings matching historical Tuscan herbals (e.g., Materia Medica).

Merits & Arguments
  • Systematic, repeatable process spanning the entire botanical section.
  • Matches carbon-dating timeframe (Tuscany, c. 1400s).
  • Includes diacritic analysis of loops and trailing curls.
Criticisms & Objections
  • The Anagram Trap: Given short strings and archaic synonyms, many Italian words can be formed, risking selection bias.
  • Unorthodox letter associations.

Tucker & O'Neill

Botanists & Taxonomists
New World Botanical Model

Approach & Contributions

Visual botanical matching of illustration morphology to physical plant species. O'Neill identified European wild pansies and sunflowers (f93r); Tucker argued for Mesoamerican origins, identifying Aztec plants (e.g., Xiuhhamolli).

Merits & Arguments
  • Provides objective physical visual markers (seed-patterns, leaf shape).
  • Attempts to resolve the identity of drawings using standard botanical keys.
Criticisms & Objections
  • Post-Columbus dating (post-1492) directly contradicts carbon dating of the vellum (1404–1438).
  • Stylized drawings are often composite medieval fantasy flora.

Newbold & Strong

Historical Cryptanalysts (Early 20th C.)
Micrography & Complex Ciphers

Approach & Contributions

William Newbold proposed that the letters are composed of tiny shorthand strokes representing Greek shorthand. Leonell Strong proposed a double-system substitution cipher using a complex modular key grid.

Merits & Arguments
  • Extremely detailed and complex mathematical decryption structures.
  • Formulated early comprehensive transcription maps of the script.
Criticisms & Objections
  • Microscopic analysis of ink showed that the "shorthand strokes" were simply cracks in the aging ink and vellum.
  • Decryption steps were too complex, allowing subjective derivation of any text.

Lisa Fagin Davis

Paleographer / Manuscript Scholar
Scribal & Codicological Model

Approach & Contributions

Analyzes the manuscript as a physical medieval artifact created by multiple scribes. Identified 5 distinct scribal hands (Scribes 1-5) and scriptorium characteristics, and extensively debunked amateur translations (e.g. Gibbs, Cheshire).

Merits & Arguments
  • Grounded in empirical medieval studies and quantitative stroke analysis.
  • Explains structural and layout shifts without relying on cipher assumptions.
  • Accounts for medieval parchment creation and page arrangement patterns.
Criticisms & Objections
  • Does not attempt to decode the language or propose a semantic translation of the text itself.
  • Relies on hand-analysis stylistics (some argue shifts could represent ink changes or wear on a single scribe's pen).
